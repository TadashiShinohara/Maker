The Decision Making Tool for Capital Investments

Assumptions

Tips

Initial Invetment - An initial purchase amount of new capital investments.

Cost of Capital - How much (8%, 10% etc...) do creditors and investors expect from new investments? (eg. after-tax interest rates on borrowings/investors' expected return) If you're not sure about the Cost of Capital of your investment, check the expected return by industries.

Investment Term - How long do new capital investments generate economic benefits? 5 years? 10 years?

Corporate Tax Rate - About 40% in the U.S.

Key Factors

Tips

Sales/Cost - How much do sales and cost increase ANNUALLY from the new capital investments? For example, you purchase a $1,000,000 machine, and the machine increases annual Sales and Cost by $500,000 and $100,000, respectively. Then, please input 500000 and 100000.

Depreciation - ANNUAL depreciation expense from new capital investments. Method of Depreciation - Right now, Straight-Line Depreciation is the only method for the app.

Residual Value - Cash value on disposal

Profit/Loss = Initial Amount - (Accumulated Depreciation + Residual Value)
Profit -(Cash Outflow), Loss +(Cash Inflow)

Annual Cash Flows - Before Tax

Calculation

Check your assumption inputs again!

Key Factors (Initial Investment, Cost of Capital etc.) - Must be number and GREATER THAN ZERO.

Cash Flows (Sales, Cost etc.) EXCEPT FOR Profit/Loss - Must be number and EQUAL TO or GREATER THAN ZERO. Input 0 if you don't have the numbers.
Profit/Loss can be either positive or negative.

Calculation Results

Tips

Net Present Value =
Discounted Cash Flows - Initial Amount
It should be positive for a good investment. If it's negative, you're losing money!

Profitabiliy Index = Discounted Cash Flows / Initial Amount
*N/A will be shown if there's no cash flow.

IRR - Greater than the Cost of Capital? Then, it's worth it!
*N/A will be shown if there's no cash flow.

Comment

Go

Your capital investment looks great. Net Present Value looks good, Profitability Index looks good, and IRR is greater than Cost of Capital. Just go for it!

No Go

Your capital investment doesn't look good. Isn't the purchase amount too high? Does the new investment really improve your cash flows? Or do sales increase enough? Please reconsider it and conduct further investigation for the implementation of the new capital investment.
